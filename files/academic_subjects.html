Intellectual Development

Note: For detailed information about specific classes—including activities, lessons, and topics in Art, Music, Spanish, or Science—please visit the Enrichment Programs page.

Math Learning

Math skills are incorporated into daily play activities. Children learn concepts in a hands-on, playful context. Examples include:

  • Identifying shapes, sizes, patterns, and sequences during play
  • Sorting and categorizing objects into sets (toys, blocks, natural items)
  • Counting and comparing objects, developing one-to-one correspondence
  • Measuring length and weight using child-friendly tools
  • Using numbers as symbols and to represent quantity in games and activities
  • Graphing observations, such as tracking daily weather or class projects

Language Development

Language learning happens naturally through play and interaction. Examples include:

  • Verbally communicating and listening to others
  • Dramatic play
  • Listening to and telling stories
  • Listening to music and singing songs
  • Identifying meaningful words – own name, friends’ names, calendar words, job chart, etc.
  • Dictating, drawing, and writing stories, and creating books of the child’s own making

Science Concepts

Science concepts are explored through observation, experimentation, and hands-on activities. Examples include:

  • Observing seasonal changes related to weather, plant life, animal behavior, and family activities
  • Sorting and categorizing items by properties like color, size, or texture
  • Collecting and observing plants, animals, and insects in the environment
  • Learning basic conservation and recycling practices
  • Growing plants from seeds and bulbs, observing stages of growth and parts of plants
  • Exploring animals through hatching eggs, butterflies, tadpoles, and more
  • Discovering how magnets work
  • Observing changes in matter with simple experiments using heat and cold
  • Cooking and eating healthy snacks

Social Studies Concepts

  • Learning about similarities and differences among peers and their families
  • Inviting family members to visit school and share cultural traditions, teach words in other languages, or read books to the class
  • Exploring the community through field trips on the Washington University campus and surrounding neighborhood
  • Learning about occupations through books, visits, and discussions with various professionals
  • Experiencing multicultural customs through hands-on activities with cultural materials
  • Learning about disabilities and respecting differences, understanding how people deal with challenges
  • Role-playing about families, occupations, abilities, or traditions
